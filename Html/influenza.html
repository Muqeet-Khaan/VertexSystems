Influenza (Flu)

Overview

Introduction:

Influenza, commonly known as the flu, is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses. It affects the nose, throat, and sometimes the lungs. Seasonal flu outbreaks occur every year and can range from mild to severe.

Cause:

The flu is caused by influenza viruses — Type A, B, and C. Among these, Type A and Type B are the most responsible for seasonal outbreaks, while Type C usually causes only mild illness.

Types of Influenza Viruses

Influenza viruses are classified into Type A, Type B, and Type C. Among them, Type A and B cause most seasonal outbreaks, while Type C usually results in only mild illness.

  • Most common and most dangerous influenza type.
  • Found in both humans and animals (birds, pigs, horses, etc.).
  • Responsible for major epidemics and pandemics (e.g., 1918 Spanish Flu, 2009 Swine Flu).
  • Changes rapidly through antigenic drift and antigenic shift.
  • Examples: H1N1, H3N2.
  • Found only in humans (not animals).
  • Causes seasonal flu outbreaks together with Type A.
  • Illness is generally less severe than Type A.
  • Mutates more slowly → no pandemics, but regular epidemics.
  • Lineages: Victoria and Yamagata.
  • Rare and generally mild.
  • Causes only mild respiratory illness (cold-like).
  • Not linked with epidemics or pandemics.
  • Mostly affects children.

Structure / Nature of Influenza Virus

Influenza viruses are RNA viruses belonging to the Orthomyxoviridae family. They have a lipid envelope with surface proteins (hemagglutinin and neuraminidase) that help the virus enter and exit host cells.

  • RNA Genome: Segmented, single-stranded RNA.
  • Surface Proteins: Hemagglutinin (H) & Neuraminidase (N).
  • Shape: Spherical/filamentous, ~80–120 nm.

Transmission

Airborne droplets

Coughing, sneezing, or even talking releases virus particles.

Close contact

Hugging, handshakes, or being near an infected person.

Surface contamination

Virus survives on surfaces (doorknobs, phones) and spreads when you touch your face.

Crowded/closed spaces

Offices, schools, and public transport increase risk.

Zoonotic transmission

Spread from animals (birds, pigs) to humans.

Common Symptoms

Fever

Cough

Sore throat

Fatigue

Runny nose

Shortness of breath

Headache

Body aches

Diseases / Complications

The flu can cause mild illness but may also lead to severe complications. These range from temporary infections to life-threatening conditions.

Mild / Moderate Complications
  • Sinus infections
  • Ear infections
  • Bronchitis
Severe Complications
  • Pneumonia (viral/bacterial)
  • Worsening of chronic illnesses (Asthma, Heart disease, Diabetes)
  • Encephalitis (brain inflammation, rare)
  • Multi-organ failure (rare, very severe)
High-Risk Groups

Some people are more likely to develop serious complications from the flu:

  • Older adults (65+)
  • Young children (<5 years)
  • Pregnant women
  • People with weak immune systems
  • Chronic illnesses (asthma, diabetes, COPD, heart disease)

Prevention

1
Annual flu vaccination

The most effective way to prevent seasonal flu.

2
Good hand hygiene

Wash hands regularly with soap and water or use sanitizer.

3
Masks & covering coughs

Wear masks and cover mouth/nose while coughing or sneezing.

4
Avoid close contact

Keep distance from sick individuals to reduce risk.

5
Disinfect surfaces

Clean commonly touched items like phones, doorknobs, keyboards.

6
Stay home when sick

Avoid spreading flu by resting at home until you recover.

Treatment

General Care
  • Rest and plenty of sleep 🛌
  • Stay hydrated with water, soups, and fluids 💧
  • Over-the-counter medicines for fever, cough, and pain 💊
Antiviral Medications

Antiviral drugs such as Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) may be prescribed for high-risk or severe cases. They work best if started within 48 hours of symptoms.

Severe Cases

Hospitalization may be required for complications like pneumonia or breathing difficulties. Treatment includes:

  • Oxygen therapy
  • IV fluids
  • Close monitoring
Antibiotics do not treat influenza, as it is a viral disease.

History of Influenza

1580

First recorded influenza pandemic swept across Europe, Asia, and Africa, marking the earliest known global outbreak.

1918

The Spanish Flu pandemic infected about one-third of the world’s population, causing tens of millions of deaths.

1930s

Scientists first isolated the influenza virus, paving the way for vaccines and antiviral research.

2009

The H1N1 “Swine Flu” pandemic emerged, leading to widespread vaccination campaigns worldwide.

Today

Annual flu vaccines and advanced surveillance continue to control outbreaks and protect global health.
